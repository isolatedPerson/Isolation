Isolation

Delve into the multifaceted experience of isolation through stories, reflections, and photographs. Inspired by the the Covid pandemic and "The Plague" by Albert Camus.

My Story

  • After reading "The Plague" by Albert Camus, I connected to my experiences during the Covid pandemic.
  • Reading about the town of Oran and how everyone was trying to cope with the disease made me see our own struggles in a new light. We were all trying to stay close to each other even when we had to be apart. This website came from that feeling.
  • I've put together pictures that show what Camus's book and our own experiences with the pandemic made me feel. I hope that as you look and listen, you'll see some of your own life here too. And I hope it helps us remember that we're all in this together.

Visual and Audio

  • Mood: The image’s moody atmosphere resonates with the emotional tone of "The Plague," where the fog of uncertainty and the weight of the situation are ever-present, just as the international student feels the weight of new surroundings and the fog of uncertainty in a new culture.
Transcript

The image of a person in an empty room with a view of the bustling New York City outside the window resonates deeply with Kiriti's interview and the themes in Albert Camus' "The Plague." The figure's stillness amidst the city's motion reflects Kiriti's interpretation of isolation as a lack of contact with others, while the room signifies the personal space where one might retreat to experience solitude or enforced isolation.
In "The Plague," the town of Oran is cut off from the world, its inhabitants isolated within its walls, much like the figure is isolated within the confines of the room. The blurred cityscape suggests the world moving on, indifferent to the individual's plight, mirroring the indifference of nature to human suffering that Camus portrays in his novel. The pandemic mentioned in the interview brings an abstract layer to the understanding of isolation. Much like the citizens of Oran, people worldwide experienced isolation during the COVID-19 lockdowns. The pandemic, as in the novel, forces a confrontation with the self and with the fundamental need for connection, which Kiriti hints at when discussing the normalization of being alone.

Help and Support

If you or someone you know is struggling with feelings of isolation, help is available. Below are some resources where you can find support.

  • National Helpline: 1-800-662-HELP (4357)

    A confidential, free, 24/7, 365-day-a-year treatment referral and information service.

  • Crisis Text Line: Text "HELLO" to 741741

    Free, 24/7 support at your fingertips.

  • Mental Health America: www.mhanational.org
